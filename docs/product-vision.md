# Picks Leagues — Product Vision

## In one line

A home for sports prognosticators. Free. Ad-free. Independent. Built by people who play.

---

## Why this exists

Pick'em pools are one of the best things about being a sports fan. They turn an ordinary Sunday into something you have stakes in. They give a group of friends a reason to talk all week. And they're one of the few competitive sports formats that doesn't require fantasy lineup management, athletic ability, or money to participate.

The platforms most pools live on today are not great. The big ones — Yahoo, ESPN — are ad-cluttered, slow to evolve, and have product surfaces that haven't meaningfully changed in years. The dedicated pool sites are often clunky, dated, or paywall the features that should be table stakes. None of them feel like they're built by people who actually run pools.

This app exists because the founder wanted to run a pick'em league for friends and couldn't find a platform that felt right. So he built Picks Leagues — with the rules he wanted, the experience he wanted, and a commitment to keep it that way.

---

## Who this is for

**Primary audience (today): friend groups.** Small groups of people — work friends, college friends, families — who already do a pool or want to start one. They want it to be easy to set up, easy to participate in, and fun to argue about all week. They are the founder and his friends; they are the people most like the founder.

**Broader audience (over time): any sports fan who wants to prove they're the best prognosticator in their circle.** The app-wide competitions exist for this audience — players who don't have a friend group on Picks Leagues but still want to compete, climb a leaderboard, and see how their picks stack up.

**Not for:**
- People looking for daily fantasy or lineup-based games. There are great apps for that; this is not one of them.
- People looking for a sportsbook. Picks here are about competition, not gambling against a house.
- People looking for fantasy football leagues. Drafts, trades, and lineup management are a different product entirely.
- Bots, syndicates, or anyone trying to game the system rather than play it.

---

## What sets this apart

Four commitments define what Picks Leagues is and what it isn't.

**1. Always free to play.** Every game mode has a free version. No paywalls on core features. No "premium tier" gating things that should be standard. Free isn't a freemium hook — it's a permanent commitment to the players.

**2. No ads, ever.** Pick'em apps that cover their costs with advertising inevitably shape the product around ad impressions: more page views, more clicks, more pop-ups blocking what players actually came to do. Picks Leagues will not sell player attention to third parties. Revenue comes from services players opt into and from sponsorships that fit the moment — never from advertisers paying to interrupt the player experience.

The distinction matters. "Ads" means third-party advertisers paying for player attention, served programmatically as banners or interstitials, where the advertiser's interests don't align with the player's. "Sponsorships" means specific, contextual partnerships that fit the surface they appear on — a local brewery sponsoring a Sunday pool of the week, a regional partner for a Super Bowl edition, a podcast partner getting a callout in a weekly recap. Done well, sponsorships add to the player experience rather than degrading it. They're the difference between *brought to you by* and *interrupted by*.

**3. Community-driven and independent.** This is not a venture-backed product on a growth-at-all-costs trajectory. Decisions are made based on what players actually want. Independence means staying small enough to listen, owning the product end to end, and not being beholden to investors, acquirers, or advertisers whose interests differ from the players'.

**4. Built by someone who plays.** The founder uses Picks Leagues in his own pool. Every product decision starts with: would I want this? Would my friends? That's a sharper filter than any focus group.

These commitments aren't marketing claims; they're constraints on every product decision. They rule out a lot of growth strategies that other apps take for granted. That's the point.

---

## How it should feel

Casual and approachable. The rules engine underneath is precise — pushes, ties, alt lines, lock multipliers, tiebreaker margins. The surface a player sees should hide that complexity unless they want it. Submitting picks should feel like a few quick taps, not filing a tax return. Joining a league should feel like accepting an invite, not navigating a settings menu.

The vibe is *Sunday afternoon with friends*, not *spreadsheet at the office*. Banter, ribbing, and bragging rights are the product's natural emotional output. Anything in the design that gets in the way of that — friction, jargon, clutter — is wrong, even if it's technically correct.

Visually: clean, readable, unfussy. No clip-art mascots, no flashing scoreboards, no over-designed empty states. The information is the product; let it breathe.

---

## What success looks like

**At end of first NFL season:** the founder's friend group runs its entire Pick'em League on the app. They prefer it to whatever they used last year. One or two adjacent friend groups have also moved over. The whole regular season runs without major scoring bugs or downtime during big games.

**Over the next two to three seasons:** dozens to hundreds of leagues run on it. Word of mouth between friend groups is the primary growth channel. Players who try it stay through a full season more often than not.

**Long-term, if everything works:** Picks Leagues becomes the obvious place to run a pick'em league. It funds itself sustainably through a mix of opt-in services (transaction fees on paid pools, optional supporter tiers, league-level perks) and contextual sponsorships, without ever introducing ads or compromising the free tier. The founder can dedicate more time to it because it generates real income — but the product doesn't change shape to chase that income.

What success is *not*: viral growth, user acquisition through paid channels, optimizing for daily active users to satisfy investors, or being acquired by a larger media company. None of those align with the commitments above.

## Long-term direction

The MVP launches with four NFL game modes (Pick'em League first, then Elimination, then App-Wide Pick'em, then H2H). From there, the product expands along two axes.

**More game modes.** The existing product spec covers Win Total Pools, Franchise Pools, March Madness brackets, and an app-wide bracket competition. All of these are planned. New game modes will continue to be added based on what players ask for and what fits the platform's character. Pick'em-style competitions for other sports (basketball, baseball, hockey, soccer) are natural extensions.

**Revenue model.** Picks Leagues will be sustained by a mix of revenue streams, all of which share one property: they align with player interests rather than working against them.

- **Transaction fees on paid pools (post-MVP).** Once the free product is stable, the app will facilitate money exchanging hands between players in three forms: league-level buy-ins where the commissioner sets a stake and the app handles collection and payouts; head-to-head matches with optional stakes between consenting players; and app-wide paid pools where everyone who buys in competes for a shared pot. The app takes a cut of each transaction. Real-money features bring real complexity — payment processing, tax reporting, state-by-state legal compliance — so they will be added carefully and only when the free product is mature enough to justify the work.
- **Optional supporter / pro tier.** Cosmetic features, custom league branding, advanced personal stats, season-long history exports, larger league size caps. Nothing that affects competition or gates core gameplay — just features some players will pay for, partly because they want them and partly because they like supporting the platform.
- **Contextual sponsorships.** Specific, opt-in partnerships that fit the surface they appear on. Done well, these add to the experience rather than degrading it.

**What won't be added:** advertising (in the traditional sense of selling player attention to third parties), sportsbook integration, daily fantasy lineup formats, traditional fantasy leagues with drafts and trades, AI-recommended picks (at least not in any form that diminishes the player's own judgment), or any feature that requires the app to take a side against its own players.

## Guiding principles for decisions

When in doubt about a product question, return to these:

1. **Players come first.** Not advertisers (there are none), not investors (there are none), not growth metrics. The question is always: does this make the product better for the people playing?
2. **Free to play is the floor, not the ceiling.** Adding paid features is fine. Putting things behind paywalls that should be free is not.
3. **Quality over scale.** Better to run 100 leagues well than 10,000 leagues poorly. Better to ship one game mode that works perfectly than four that mostly work.
4. **Casual surface, precise engine.** The rules can be as elaborate as the product spec requires; the UI should never make a casual player feel that complexity.
5. **Independence is a feature, not a constraint.** Staying small and self-funded is what makes the other commitments possible. Don't trade it away for short-term growth.
6. **Listen, then decide.** Player feedback is the primary input. But the founder still owns the decision — listening doesn't mean shipping every request.

## Open questions

These don't need answers now but should be revisited as the product matures:

- **What does the paid-pool product look like specifically?** League buy-ins, peer-to-peer stakes, and app-wide pools each have different mechanics, different compliance requirements, and different appeal. Worth thinking through carefully before building, particularly around how the app holds and pays out funds, what KYC/tax reporting looks like, and which states are addressable.
- **What does the supporter tier actually include?** The principle is clear (nothing that affects competition) but the specifics matter. The right answer becomes clearer once there's a real user base asking for specific features.
- **How does Picks Leagues fund itself before paid-pool features exist?** First season is a personal-and-friends project; hosting costs are minimal. As leagues grow, basic costs grow. A supporter tier could launch before paid pools as a way to bridge. Or absorb the cost personally for the first year. No urgent answer needed, but worth deciding before it becomes urgent.
- **At what scale does "independent" need defending more actively?** Right now, independence is easy because the app is small. If growth happens, acquisition offers and investor interest will follow. Worth deciding in advance what the answer to those conversations is.
